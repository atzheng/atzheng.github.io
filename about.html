About me

1. Bio

I’m Andy Zheng, a PhD student at the Operations Research Center at MIT, advised by Prof. Vivek Farias. I’m interested in experimentation in marketplace settings, specifically challenges relating to interference and sequential designs.

Previously, I was a data scientist at Uber, working on match optimization for UberPOOL. I recieved a B.S. in Industrial Engineering and an M.S. in Computer Science at Northwestern.

2. Publications

  • Markovian Interference in Experiments (2022, Preprint) with Vivek Farias, Andrew A. Li, and Tianyi Peng.

    We model experimentation in marketplace settings as an off-policy evaluation (OPE) problem. On one hand, “naive” A/B testing suffers bias on the order of the treatment effect; on the other, unbiased OPE estimators suffer from extremely high variance. We develop a bias-corrected estimator with bias second-order in the treatment effect, and variance exponentially smaller than unbiased OPE. This estimator dramatically improves over alternatives, even in our experiments on a large-scale ridesharing simulator. This correction, is derive from a Taylor-like expansion of the off-policy objective value, which is of independent interest.

    Finalist, 2022 INFORMS APS Best Student Paper Award.

  • Synthetically Controlled Bandits (2022, Preprint) with Vivek Farias, Ciamac Moallemi, and Tianyi Peng.

    Synthetic controls are commonly used to control for non-stationarity, but the fixed experimental designs typical in such settings are both costly and fragile to non-stationarity in the controls. By instead designing experiments adaptively, we can robustly identify the optimal treatment while incurring low regret.

    Presented at RMP 2022, MSOM Service Management SIG 2022.

  • The Limits to Learning a Diffusion Process (2020, Preprint) with Jackie Baek, Vivek Farias, Andreea Georgescu, Retsef Levi, Tianyi Peng, Deeksha Sinha, and Joshua Wilde.

    Diffusion processes model viral transmission in a population. Two classic examples are the SIR model for disease transmission, and the Bass model for product adoption. We present a Cramer-Rao bound characterizing the difficulty of learning such models in a stochastic setting. Key parameters cannot be learned with reasonable variance until 2/3 of the time where infections (or product adoptions) are at their peak, a major barrier for e.g. timely pandemic response. In practice, we work around this via a regularization approach based on geographic heterogeneity, and apply this approach to predicting the spread of COVID-19.

    Presented at EC 2021. Under Review at Management Science.

  • Optimizing Offer Sets in Sublinear Time (2020, Preprint) with Vivek Farias, Andrew A. Li, and Deeksha Sinha.

    Online retailers scale to millions of products and users. Systems at this scale must be able to provide high-quality product recommendations in milliseconds, necessitating approaches to assortment optimization that work in time sub-linear in the number of products. We design a sampling scheme, based on locality-sensitive hashing, which samples a set of candidate products which is 1) sublinear in size, and 2) optimizing over which yields an assortment.

    Under review at Management Science.

  • Non-parametric Approximate Dynamic Programming via the Kernel Method (2022) with Nikhil Bhat, Vivek Farias, and Ciamac Moallemi. To appear in Management Science.

  • Computing Estimators of Dantzig Selector type via Column and Constraint Generation (2019, Preprint) with Rahul Mazumder and Stephen Wright.

    Fast algorithms for the Dantzig selector and related problems, using large-scale LP techniques and warm starts.
